CORE SKILLS

The 6 Core Skills

These six skills are specifically assessed through the ESP p.9. They are embedded throughout the two-year programme and must be demonstrated in context within the project tasks — not simply defined.

01 · Be Able to Reflectively Evaluate

Review your own work critically against the brief requirements and success criteria. Identify specifically what worked, what didn't, and what you would do differently if revisiting the problem. Reference test evidence. Avoid vague statements like "it could be improved" — say how and why.

PRE-RELEASE

Pre-Release Task

Not directly assessed p.49 — but students can use their research to inform responses to the assessed tasks. The pre-release allows familiarisation with the industry context of the main brief (e.g. use of digital solutions in the financial sector).

What You Should Do

  1. Carry out independent investigation into the given industry context — research how digital systems are used in that sector, key organisations, common challenges and relevant legislation
  2. Share findings with others — discuss what you've found, compare sources, identify gaps and contradictions
  3. Work in groups — collaborate on organising and presenting research; practise assigning tasks by skill
  4. Take part in and lead discussions — articulate your findings clearly, listen to others, and build a shared understanding of the context

Why It Matters

Even though it's not assessed directly, strong pre-release research:

  • Gives you real examples to reference in your rationale and evaluation
  • Helps you understand the business context so your planning and design decisions are grounded
  • Develops your collaborative skills (Core Skill 3) ahead of the assessed tasks
  • Prepares you to discuss stakeholder needs in your design documents

What Good Research Looks Like

Research AreaUseful things to find out
Industry contextWho the main organisations are; what digital systems they use; what problems they face
StakeholdersWho the users are; what they need; internal vs external audiences
LegislationRelevant laws (GDPR for financial data, FCA regulations, H&S); how these affect software development in that sector
SecurityCommon security threats in the sector; what mitigations are used
Digital systemsTypes of systems used; hardware/software requirements; data flows
Business modelsWhether it's B2B, B2C; who the clients and customers are
💡 Practical tip: Take organised notes during your pre-release research with clear headings. You may be able to refer to these during your assessed sessions (check with your centre). Even if not, the understanding you build directly improves every task from planning to evaluation.
TASK 1 · PLANNING

Task 1 — Planning a Project

Task 1 has three parts: a Gantt chart, a Resource & Cost Plan, and a written Rationale. Use the Pearson-provided template. p.49–50

Gantt Chart

A visual project schedule showing all tasks, their durations, sequencing and who is responsible.

  • Assess the strengths and skills of team members and assign appropriate tasks to them — justify your assignment choices
  • Make scheduling decisions based on a defined deadline — identify the critical path
  • Prioritise activities based on analysis of requirements
  • Show how resources are correctly assigned to each task
  • Use an appropriate software development lifecycle (SDLC) to organise tasks (e.g. Agile, Waterfall, Iterative)
  • Show task dependencies — which tasks must finish before others can start

Resource & Cost Plan

A breakdown of all resources required and their associated costs.

  • Identify and calculate costs for:
    • Materials (software licences, consumables)
    • Physical resources (hardware, equipment)
    • Personnel (staff time × hourly rate × days)
  • Select and allocate resources to a resource list
  • Correctly attribute costs to provide an accurate total project cost estimate
  • Determine the affordability and viability of implementing the project
  • Consider the impact on the company over time — ongoing costs vs one-off costs

Rationale

A written justification of the most significant planning decisions made.

  • Consider the factors most relevant when planning projects
  • Justify decisions about:
    • Order and timing of tasks — why this sequence?
    • Allocation of team members — why this person for this role?
    • Potential benefits and risks — what could go wrong?
    • Impact on timings and costs — what are the knock-on effects?
  • Use SMARTER objectives where relevant: Specific · Measurable · Achievable · Realistic · Time-bound · Evaluated · Reviewed
⚠️ High-mark rationale: Don't just describe what you did — justify why. Compare options. E.g. "I used a phased implementation approach rather than direct implementation because the financial sector requires continuous service availability, and a direct cutover would risk service disruption for clients. The phased approach adds 3 days to the timeline but reduces the risk of reputational damage."

Choose and justify an SDLC in your Gantt chart. Each has different task organisation implications:

SDLC ModelHow tasks are organisedBest for

SMARTER Objectives p.29

Use SMARTER objectives when identifying the changes to be made and when setting project goals in your rationale.

Example SMARTER Objective for an ESP Project

"Develop a Python function that validates customer account numbers against a check digit algorithm, processes a minimum of 200 accounts per second, passes all 8 unit tests including boundary values, and is integrated and tested by the end of day 3 of the project. Progress will be reviewed at the end of each day session."

This is Specific (function, check digit), Measurable (200/sec, 8 tests), Achievable, Realistic (within a day's work), Time-bound (end of day 3), and reviewable.

TASK 2 · DEBUGGING

Task 2 — Identifying & Fixing Defects

Task 2 has two parts: (1) documenting the testing process and (2) fixing the code. Both are assessed. p.50

Part 1 — Documenting the Testing Process

Provide annotated evidence of testing that includes all of the following:

  1. Identify tests to be carried out — systematically plan which aspects of the code to test before running anything
  2. Describe the purpose of each identified test — what does this test prove or disprove?
  3. Identify test data for each test, including all five types: valid · valid extreme · invalid · invalid extreme · erroneous
  4. Describe expected results — what should the code output for each test input, before you run it?
  5. Record actual results — what did the code actually output when tested?
  6. Compare actual vs expected — identify discrepancies that indicate a defect
  7. Describe further actions required — what changes will you make to fix each identified defect?
  8. Refine the system as required — implement the fixes and re-test

Part 2 — The Solution (Fixed Code)

  • Correct all errors in the provided code so that it is functional and meets the given requirements
  • Add or remove code where necessary — don't just patch symptoms; fix the underlying logic
  • Justify every decision made when fixing a defect — explain what was wrong and why your fix is correct
  • Follow appropriate programming conventions when fixing code:
    • Maintain consistent naming conventions throughout
    • Use precise logic — don't introduce unnecessary complexity
    • Maintain proper indentation and code structure
    • Add comments to explain changes made
  • Ensure the program produces consistently correct outcomes — test edge cases, not just the happy path

The Five Types of Test Data p.50

TypeDefinitionExample (age field, valid 0–120)
ValidCorrect data within the acceptable range — should be accepted25
Valid extremeCorrect data at the very edge of the acceptable range0 and 120
InvalidIncorrect data outside the acceptable range — should be rejected-5 or 150

⚠️ Common mistake: Many candidates only test with valid data and one or two invalid values. To score highly, you must include all five types and show you planned the tests before running them. Annotate your evidence — don't just paste code output with no explanation.
TASK 2 · DEBUGGING

Types of Errors & Debugging Approach

Syntax Errors

The code cannot run at all — it breaks the rules of the Python language.

# BEFORE — syntax error
def calculate_tax(price rate):
    return price * rate

# AFTER — fixed (missing comma)
def calculate_tax(price, rate):
    return price * rate

Python's interpreter will point to the line. Check: missing colons, unmatched brackets, misspelled keywords.

Logic Errors

The code runs but produces wrong output — the algorithm is incorrect.

# BEFORE — logic error
def is_adult(age):
    if age > 18:   # wrong: > not >=
        return True
    return False

# AFTER — fixed
def is_adult(age):
    if age >= 18:   # correct
        return True
    return False

Use a trace table — step through with test data, tracking each variable's value manually.

Runtime Errors

The code crashes during execution when given unexpected input.

# BEFORE — crashes on bad input
def get_age():
    age = int(input("Age: "))
    return age

# AFTER — handled robustly
def get_age():
    try:
        age = int(input("Age: "))
        return age
    except ValueError:
        print("Enter a whole number")
        return None

Use try/except to handle errors gracefully and produce meaningful messages.

Systematic Debugging Process

  1. Read the requirements carefully — understand what the code is supposed to do before testing
  2. Plan your tests — choose test data covering all five types before running anything
  3. Trace the code manually for simple cases — use a trace table tracking each variable
  4. Run tests and record actual results alongside expected results
  5. Identify the root cause — not just the symptom; understand why it fails
  6. Fix and re-test — after each fix, re-run all tests to check for regression

TASK 3 · DESIGN

Task 3 — Designing a Solution

Task 3 is assessed on: decomposition quality · application of logical thinking · correct use of conventions · communication of the design. p.50–51

Decomposition of the Problem p.50

  • Break the problem into smaller parts suitable for computational solutions — justify every decision made
  • Make effective use of detailed abstraction and refinement — identify inputs, outputs, what varies, what's constant, key actions, repeated processes
  • Use elements of reusable components where appropriate — reference existing functions or modules
  • Use good decomposition to show all the necessary sub-systems that make up the full solution
  • Use appropriate tools for communicating algorithms: flowcharts and/or code (pseudocode or Python)

Application of Logical Thinking p.51

  • Describe the parts of the solution using algorithms
  • Clearly define every step — no ambiguity
  • Uniquely define each step — steps should depend on the input and result of preceding steps
  • Ensure the algorithm makes use of all three key constructs: sequence · selection · iteration
Worked example: A login system algorithm must show: sequence (get username → get password), selection (if credentials valid → grant access, else → increment attempts), and iteration (while attempts < MAX_ATTEMPTS, repeat the check).

Use of Conventions p.51

Use correct structure and convention for your chosen method:

If using Flowcharts
Use correct symbols: Oval = Terminator · Rectangle = Process · Double-border rectangle = Sub-process · Diamond = Decision · Parallelogram = Input/Output · Arrows with labels = flow
Label all decision branches (Yes/No or True/False)
Every flowchart must have a START and END terminator
If using Code / Pseudocode
Use consistent naming conventions — snake_case or camelCase throughout
Proper indentation to show code structure
Use meaningful function and variable names

Communication of the Design p.51

Design documents must be detailed enough to:

  • Effectively communicate the intended solution to all readers
  • Allow the client to make informed decisions about the project
  • Allow a third party to build the proposed solution from your documents alone — without asking you questions

Use an appropriate combination of written and diagrammatical presentation. Use appropriate technical vocabulary — adjust for your audience. Provide explanations of structures and processes.

⚠️ Common mistake: Vague designs that say "the system will validate user input" without specifying what data is input, what validation checks are applied, and what happens when validation fails. If a developer can't build it from your design, it will score poorly.
TASK 4A · DEVELOP

Task 4a — Developing a Solution

Task 4a is the highest-stakes task in the ESP. A working solution that meets all requirements demonstrates all six core skills simultaneously. p.51–52

The Solution — Requirements

  • Apply programming skills to develop a solution that meets all requirements of the brief
  • Refine the system as required — it does not need to be perfect first time, but must be functional
  • Demonstrate an appropriate level of technical skill — don't just use print statements; use functions, modules, data structures
  • Use pre-written and user-written modules with appropriate interfaces

Code Organisation Standards p.51

StandardWhat it means in practice
No excessive nestingAvoid multiple pages of nested if-clauses and for-loops. Use functions to break logic into manageable units.
Clear meaningful indentation4 spaces per level in Python. Consistent throughout — don't mix tabs and spaces.
Precise logicUse functions, classes or objects with proper structure. One function = one responsibility.
CommentsComment every function (purpose, params, return value). Comment complex or non-obvious logic.
Local over global variablesDeclare variables inside functions. Use globals minimally and only when unavoidable. Use constants for values that never change.
Well-designed interfaceConsistent UI — clear prompts, formatted output, meaningful error messages.
Consistent styleSame naming convention, same indentation style, same comment format throughout.
Defensive programmingValidate all inputs before using them. Never assume the user enters correct data.
Secure data handlingDon't expose sensitive data in output. Hash passwords. Don't hard-code credentials.
Exception handlingUse try/except blocks — produce specific, meaningful error messages.

User Experience Requirements p.52

  • Consistency — the interface behaves predictably; menus always appear in the same format
  • Simplification of user input — e.g. use menus instead of free text where possible; provide clear prompts that tell the user exactly what to enter
  • Meaningful output messages — results explained in context; error messages say what went wrong and what to do instead
  • Data visualisation — use Matplotlib to produce graphs of results where appropriate; show trends and patterns

Code Quality Example

# ✅ HIGH QUALITY — follows all standards
MAX_ATTEMPTS = 3    # constant

def validate_account_number(account_num):
    """Validates an 8-digit account number.
    Returns (True, int) or (False, error_msg)."""
    try:
        num = int(account_num)      # type check
        if len(str(num)) != 8:     # length check
            return False, "Must be exactly 8 digits"
        return True, num
    except ValueError:
        return False, "Account number must be numeric"

def get_account_number():
    """Prompts user until a valid account number is entered."""
    for attempt in range(MAX_ATTEMPTS):
        user_input = input("Enter 8-digit account number: ")
        valid, result = validate_account_number(user_input)
        if valid:
            return result
        print(f"Error: {result}. {MAX_ATTEMPTS-attempt-1} attempts left.")
    print("Maximum attempts reached.")
    return None
Note on testing in 4a: Formal test documentation is not required in Task 4a. However, you should still test as you go — the evidence of testing is implicit through a working product that meets all requirements.
TASK 4B · EVALUATE

Task 4b — Reflective Evaluation

Task 4b assesses Core Skill 1 — Reflective Evaluation. It is worth significant marks and is frequently under-answered. A strong evaluation is evidence-based, specific, and analytical. p.52

What You Must Do

  1. Apply reflection and evaluation techniques — don't just describe what you built; analyse it critically against what it was supposed to do
  2. Provide evidence that the product meets brief requirements — reference each requirement or success criterion explicitly. Quote the requirement, then show how your solution satisfies it.
  3. Include measures against success criteria — e.g. "Requirement 3 states the system must process 100 records in under 5 seconds. My testing in Task 2 showed the system processed 100 records in 2.3 seconds, exceeding this requirement."
  4. Provide evidence from testing that user needs are met — reference specific test results, not just say "it passed testing"
  5. Discuss how it could be improved if the problem was revisited — specific, justified improvements, not vague wishes

Improvement Suggestions — High vs Low Mark

❌ Low-mark improvement✅ High-mark improvement
"I could improve the user interface""The input prompts are functional but could be replaced with a menu-driven interface using numbered options, which would reduce erroneous input errors — in my testing, 3 of 8 erroneous test cases were caused by users not knowing the expected format"
"The code could be more efficient""The search function currently uses a linear search which has O(n) time complexity. With more time, I would implement a binary search on the sorted account number list, which would reduce comparisons from n to log₂(n) — significant for a financial system handling millions of accounts"
"I would add error handling""The file I/O section does not handle the case where the data file is missing or corrupted. I would add a try/except FileNotFoundError block that creates a new empty file and logs the event, preventing the program from crashing in production"

Structure of a Strong Evaluation

For each requirement: State the requirement → describe how your solution meets it → cite specific evidence (test data, test result, line of code) → note any limitations or edge cases not fully addressed.
For overall reflection: What went well and why? What would you do differently in the approach (planning, design or implementation) if starting again? What new skills did you develop?
For improvements: What are the two or three most impactful improvements? For each: describe what you would change, how you would change it (briefly), and what problem this would solve or what requirement this would better meet.

Worked Evaluation Paragraph

Requirement 2 states that the system must validate all customer account numbers before processing. My validate_account_number() function applies three checks: a type check (int() conversion with ValueError catch), a length check (len(str(num)) != 8), and a presence check. This was verified in Task 2 testing — test case 4 used the erroneous input "hello" and the system correctly returned "Account number must be numeric" without crashing (actual result matched expected result). However, the function does not currently implement a check digit algorithm to verify the account number is mathematically valid. If revisiting this project, I would implement the Luhn algorithm as an additional validation step, which would prevent fabricated account numbers that happen to be 8 digits from passing validation — an important security consideration for a financial system.

⚠️ Time tip: Don't leave 4b until the last 10 minutes. Allocate at least 45–60 minutes. Write it throughout the project if possible — note down what you discover during testing so you have specific evidence ready.

STRATEGY

Time Management & Strategy

The ESP runs over 14 hours 30 minutes across multiple supervised sessions. Managing your time strategically is as important as your technical skill.
Task 1 · Planning
~2 hours
Complete the full Gantt chart with all tasks, durations, dependencies and team assignments. Fill the resource and cost plan with calculated totals. Write a rationale that justifies your three most significant decisions. Don't over-perfect the layout — content and justification earn marks, not formatting.
Task 2 · Debugging
~3 hours
Read the code and requirements first — understand what it should do before testing anything. Plan all tests. Run them systematically. Document as you go — don't test everything then try to write it up from memory. Fix each defect, re-test, and annotate your fix with a brief justification.
Task 3 · Design
~3 hours
Decompose first — list all sub-systems before drawing anything. Then design each sub-system using flowcharts or pseudocode. Check your flowchart symbols. Make sure the design is complete enough for a developer to build from. Allocate 15 minutes to review and add missing labels, edge cases and error paths.
Task 4a · Develop
~4.5 hours
Start with the core functionality first — get it working, then add comments, validation, error handling and UX improvements. Follow your Task 3 design. If you're stuck on one feature, move to another and return. Finish 15 minutes early and review your code against the requirements checklist.
Task 4b · Evaluate
~2 hours
Write this throughout the project — make notes during testing and development of what worked and what didn't. In the final session: structure your evaluation around each requirement, cite specific test evidence, then close with two or three specific, justified improvements. Proofread for audience appropriateness.
💡 If you run out of time: A partial solution with full documentation (planning, design, testing records, evaluation) typically scores better than a "complete" solution with no documentation. The process marks are spread across all tasks — don't sacrifice them all for a slightly more complete 4a.
⚠️ Before submission: Check every requirement in the brief against your work. Check that your file names, folder structure and submission format meet Pearson's requirements. Make sure all evidence is included — a missing file loses all the marks attached to it.

CORE SKILL 6

Security in the ESP

Core Skill 6 — Ensure software development activity mitigates risks to security is assessed across Tasks 2, 3, 4a and 4b. It is not just about knowing security concepts — you must apply them in your code and design. p.9

In Task 3 — Design

  • Show input validation in your flowcharts — decision diamonds for every input with both valid and invalid paths
  • Design access control — show how the system determines who can access what
  • Show error handling paths — what happens when something unexpected occurs
  • Note any data sensitivity considerations — e.g. "password must never be displayed or stored in plaintext"
  • Consider data flow security — where data enters and exits the system and what protections apply at each point

In Task 4a — Code

Worked Security Code Example

import hashlib

def hash_password(password):
    """Returns SHA-256 hash of the password."""
    return hashlib.sha256(password.encode()).hexdigest()

def authenticate(username, password, user_db):
    """Checks credentials. Returns True if valid.
    Never reveals whether username or password was wrong."""
    hashed = hash_password(password)
    if username in user_db:
        if user_db[username] == hashed:
            return True
    print("Invalid credentials.")  # vague — intentional
    return False
💡 Security evaluation tip: The error message "Invalid credentials" is intentionally vague — this prevents an attacker from knowing whether the username or password was wrong (which would help them narrow down attacks). This type of reasoning — explaining the why behind security decisions — is exactly what the higher mark bands require.

Worked Example — Same Content, Two Audiences

Technical version (for the developer)

The validate_account function implements a presence check (if value == ""), a type check (try: int(value)), a length check (len(str(int(value))) != 8), and a check digit validation using the Luhn algorithm. It returns a tuple (bool, str) — True with the validated integer on success, or False with a descriptive error message on failure. Exceptions are caught at the ValueError level only.

Non-technical version (for the client)

When a user enters an account number, the system automatically checks that: the field has not been left blank; it contains only numbers; it is exactly 8 digits long; and it is a valid account number (using a mathematical check). If any check fails, the user sees a clear message explaining what is wrong and is prompted to try again. This ensures only genuine account numbers can be processed, protecting both the business and its customers.
