Business Environment & Models

Types of Organisation

TypeDescription & Key Features
Private — SMESmall/medium enterprise (under 250 employees); owner-managed; profit-driven; more agile but fewer resources

💡 Purpose: Organisations either provide a service (e.g. streaming, banking, insurance) or provide a product (e.g. hardware, packaged software, consumer goods). Many organisations do both — e.g. Apple provides both hardware products and cloud services.

Business Models

📪 B2C — Business to Customer

Selling directly to end consumers. Typically involves high volume, lower-value transactions. Examples: retail websites (ASOS), streaming platforms (Netflix), food delivery apps (Deliveroo), online banking.

🤝 B2B — Business to Business

Selling goods or services to other organisations. Typically lower volume but higher-value contracts. Examples: cloud hosting (AWS), payroll software (Sage), component suppliers, marketing agencies, enterprise SaaS.

📡 B2M — Business to Many

Targeting both businesses and consumers simultaneously with the same or related products. Examples: Microsoft 365 (home and enterprise), Amazon (marketplace and AWS), Google (Search and Google Cloud).

Exam tip: Be ready to identify a business model from a scenario and justify why it suits that organisation type and its customers. Consider transaction volume, customer type, and pricing model.
BUSINESS · 5.1.3

Stakeholders

Definition: A stakeholder is any individual or group with an interest in, or who is affected by, the decisions and activities of an organisation or project. Identifying stakeholders is essential before any digital project begins.

Internal Stakeholders

👑 Owners / Directors

Set strategic direction and make key decisions. Responsible for financial performance and company vision. Accountable to shareholders or, in public sector, to the government. Must approve budgets for digital projects.

👔 Managers

Oversee day-to-day operations and project delivery. Bridge between directors and employees. Must communicate change, allocate resources, and monitor progress against KPIs.

👥 Employees

Carry out day-to-day operations. Directly affected by digital system changes, new tools, and working practice shifts. Must be consulted, trained and supported during project implementation. Can be source of resistance to change if not properly engaged.

External Stakeholders

StakeholderInterest / Influence
Customers / ClientsWant quality products and reliable service; directly affected by system changes; drive demand and revenue; may require consultation on UX
SuppliersProvide materials, components or services; affected by procurement system changes; must integrate with organisation's systems
ShareholdersInvest capital; expect financial returns; influence major decisions at board level; scrutinise IT investment ROI
Outsourced servicesThird-party providers (IT support, cloud, logistics, security); need system integration; SLAs must be maintained
Investors / FundersProvide growth capital; expect accountability and measurable results; may specify use of funds
GovernmentSets regulations and legislation (GDPR, CMA); collects taxes; grants operating licences; may fund public sector projects
⚠️ Exam tip: Questions often present a digital project scenario and ask you to identify relevant stakeholders, explain their interest, and describe how they would be affected by proposed changes. Always consider both internal and external perspectives and think about conflicting stakeholder interests.

BUSINESS · 5.3

Risk to Organisations

🔒 Security Breaches (CIA)

  • Compromised confidentiality — data accessed by unauthorised parties (e.g. data leak, hacking)
  • Loss of integrity — data modified without authorisation (e.g. tampering, corruption)
  • Reduced availability — systems made inaccessible (e.g. DDoS, ransomware)

🔐 Privacy Breaches

  • Exposure of personal customer data (names, addresses, payment info)
  • Leakage of sensitive business information (contracts, IP, strategy)
  • May violate GDPR leading to significant ICO fines (up to 4% global turnover or £17.5m)

⚖️ Legal & Regulatory

  • Non-compliance with GDPR, Computer Misuse Act, Equality Act
  • Failure to meet accessibility regulations (public sector websites)
  • Breach of operating licences or industry standards (PCI DSS, ISO 27001)
  • Intellectual property infringement

👤 Audience Exclusion

  • Biases in datasets or algorithms leading to discriminatory outcomes
  • Poor user experience for elderly, users with disabilities or low digital literacy
  • Non-accessible design (no screen reader support, poor colour contrast) excludes users
  • Breaches public sector accessibility regulations

📉 Technical Issues

  • Over-reliance on a single digital system creating single point of failure
  • System failure causing operational downtime and lost revenue
  • Systems that become not fit for purpose as business needs change
  • Legacy system compatibility issues blocking upgrades

🏛️ Rival Technologies

  • Disruption by emerging competitors with newer technology
  • Obsolescence of current platforms (e.g. Flash, Internet Explorer)
  • Loss of market share to more technologically innovative rivals
  • Failure to adopt cloud/AI gives competitors efficiency advantage

Potential Impact of Risks

Legal action (civil and criminal) Regulatory fines (ICO, FCA) Reputational damage & loss of public trust Withdrawal of licence to practise Loss of business / customers Reduced security (weakened defences) Financial loss (refunds, compensation, lost contracts)
Exam tip: When asked about the impact of a risk, always give a specific example for that organisation type. For a charity: reputational damage may mean loss of donations. For a bank: legal action and FCA fines. Be specific.

Change Management — Triggers

Technical change management is the structured process of planning, implementing and reviewing changes to digital systems to minimise risk and disruption to services. Change can be triggered by factors inside or outside the organisation.

Internal Triggers (5.4.1)

  • Organisational restructuring — new departments, mergers, acquisitions
  • Expansion — growth into new markets or geographic locations
  • Downsizing — reducing staff or service scope; systems consolidation
  • New strategic objectives:
Diversification (new products/markets) Rebranding New features or services

External Triggers — PESTLE (5.4.2)

FactorExamples
PoliticalChange in government; conflict affecting supply chains; shift in government priorities (e.g. move to digital public services)
EconomicRecession; inflation; interest rate changes; consumer trend shifts; new competitors; entering new markets; provision of new services
SocialChanges in demographics; market/social trends; adapting to remote working; cultural expectations around digital services
TechnologicalEmergence of new technologies (AI, cloud); retirement of obsolete systems; system failure; zero-day vulnerabilities requiring urgent patches
LegalNew legislation (GDPR, Online Safety Act); changes to existing laws affecting data processing or accessibility
EnvironmentalSustainability requirements (carbon targets); pandemics (COVID-19 drove remote working adoption); natural disasters disrupting infrastructure

How Organisations Respond to Change (5.4.3)

New or amended policies New or amended business processes: change in staffing numbers · change in delivery schedules · change in opening hours New or amended products or services (new, next-gen, or minor updates) New or improved digital systems (back-end or customer-facing) Improved training programmes Restructuring (management structure or redrawing boundaries)
Remember: Responses can be to completely new products/services, next-generation versions of existing ones, or just minor updates. Changes can affect back-end infrastructure OR customer-facing systems — sometimes both.
BUSINESS · 5.4.3–5.4.5

Change Management — Process

Change Advisory Board (CAB)

  • Identify type of change: new system or amendment to existing system
  • Prioritise change requests by urgency and impact
  • Review requests through defined stages of approval
  • Monitor — collate and analyse change data; check implementation; take action to accelerate where needed
  • Provide feedback to stakeholders throughout the process

SMARTER Objectives

SpecificMeasurableAchievableRealisticTime-boundEvaluatedReviewed

Impact Identification

  • Measure/forecast positive and negative impacts before implementation
  • Analyse positive impacts (cost savings, productivity, new products, reputation)
  • Analyse negative impacts (disruption, resistance, cost, security risks)
  • Communicate risks and desired impacts to stakeholders to gain acceptance and ensure compliance

Resource Allocation

Budget Time Staffing Hardware & software resources

Implementation Methods

⚊ Parallel

Old and new systems run simultaneously until new system is proven. Safe but costly — double running costs. Best for critical systems where downtime is unacceptable.

🗣 Phased

Gradual rollout, module by module or department by department. Balances risk and cost well. Each phase can be evaluated before proceeding.

🔎 Pilot

New system trialled in one location or department first. Low risk; provides real-world feedback before wider rollout. Ideal for testing user acceptance.

■ Direct (Big Bang)

Old system switched off, new system switched on simultaneously. Fast and cheap but very high risk — no fallback if problems occur.

Post-Implementation

  • Configuration: integrate with legacy systems; maintain service during change
  • Testing: reproducibility of results; dedicated test environment (hardware and software)
  • Documentation: requirement traceability (responsibility & accountability); maintain up-to-date records; retain all change documentation; user training manuals
  • Training needs: identify new training requirements; plan refresher courses
  • Monitoring: post-progress review; KPI tracking
🔄 Rollback planning: Define backup methodology, backup location and recovery plan before implementing. Use version control software to track changes and enable reversion if the new system fails.

Feasibility Factors (5.4.5)

Benefits

  • Financial savings from automation
  • Cost of implementing change vs long-term savings
  • Impact on processes (productivity gains, better communication, improved security)
  • Provision of new products or services
  • Impact on company reputation

Risks

  • Resistance to change from workforce
  • Misuse of new systems by users
  • Inadequate support for new system post-deployment
  • Inadequate knowledge of new system among staff
  • Disruption caused during implementation

Constraints

  • Budget available for implementation
  • Time available (deadlines, contractual obligations)
  • Human resources (staffing levels, skills)
  • Technological resources (hardware, software, infrastructure)

DATA · 6.1

Data, Information & Knowledge

📊 Data

Raw, unprocessed facts and figures with no context. Data alone has no inherent meaning. Can be collected automatically or manually.

Examples: 42, red, 07900 123456, 2025-06-01

📋 Information

Data that has been processed, organised or contextualised so it becomes meaningful and useful for a specific purpose.

Example: "Customer age is 42; preferred colour: red; joined 1 June 2025"

🧠 Knowledge

Understanding derived from information — combining context, experience and judgement to support informed decision-making.

Example: "Customers aged 40–50 prefer classic colours — target marketing accordingly in summer campaigns."

  • Timeframe — is the data sufficiently current, or is it outdated for the purpose?
  • Source — is the origin reliable, authoritative and unbiased?
  • Veracity — how accurate, consistent and trustworthy is the data?

  • Threat/opportunity assessment — competitor analysis, security monitoring, compliance checking
DATA · 6.3

Data Taxonomy

Primary Categories (6.3.1)

🔢 Quantitative (Structured)

Numerical data that can be measured or counted. Stored in databases and spreadsheets in defined fields. Can be analysed mathematically to identify patterns and trends.

DiscreteContinuousCategorical

💬 Qualitative (Unstructured)

Descriptive, non-numerical data. Stored and retrieved only as a single object (it cannot be broken into smaller meaningful parts). Can be codified into structured data (e.g. classifying reviews as positive/negative/neutral, converting free-text to sentiment scores).

Examples: Customer reviews, images, audio recordings, social media posts, PDFs, video files.

Quantitative Data Representations (6.3.4)

TypeDefinitionExample
DiscreteCounted; can only take specific whole number values; gaps between values existNumber of items sold, age in years, number of users
ContinuousMeasured; can take any value within a range; infinite possible valuesTemperature (21.7°C), height (1.834m), weight, time
CategoricalGrouped into named classes; no numerical relationship between categoriesGender, product type, country, colour preference

Structured vs Unstructured Data (6.3.2-6.3.3)

Structured Data

Organised in rows and columns (tables). Has a defined schema. Easy to query and analyse with SQL. Quantitative data is stored as structured data. Examples: relational databases, spreadsheets.

Unstructured Data

No predefined format or schema. Stored as a single object. Harder to analyse without transformation. Qualitative data is stored as unstructured. Examples: emails, videos, images, PDFs, audio files.

Key relationship (6.3.6): Quantitative data is structured. Qualitative data is unstructured. Qualitative data can be codified (converted into structured quantitative data) — e.g. NPS score from survey text.

Data Type Relationships

Structured Data ↔ Data Type

Structured data requires defined data types per field. The choice of type affects storage space, query performance, and available operations.

Unstructured Data ↔ Data Type

Blob stores raw binary data for unstructured content (images, video). Text/string types store unstructured textual content.

Data Type ↔ Transformation

The right type enables transformation: integers support arithmetic, dates support date arithmetic, Booleans enable filtering and conditional logic.

Exam tip: Justify the best data type for a given scenario — consider: precision needed, operations to perform, storage efficiency, and whether the data is structured or unstructured.

Methods of Transforming Data (6.2)

🔧 Manipulating

Changing, filtering, sorting or restructuring data — e.g. cleaning messy input, removing duplicates, converting between formats (CSV to JSON), normalising values (e.g. all dates to ISO 8601).

📈 Analysing

Examining data to find patterns, trends and anomalies — e.g. calculating averages and totals, running statistical analysis, producing charts and dashboards for decision-making.

⚙️ Processing

Applying operations to produce useful output from raw input — e.g. running payroll calculations, generating monthly reports, applying classification algorithms to categorise data.

Ethical data practices: When transforming data ensure high veracity (accuracy), appropriate timeframe, reliable source, and sufficient quantity for valid conclusions. Interrelationship: choice of data format directly impacts ease of transformation — JSON and CSV are easier to process programmatically than XML or plain text.
DATA · 6.6 & 6.7

Data Storage Structures & Big Data

Structures for Storing Data (6.6)

📄 Metadata

Data about data. Metadata provides descriptions and context that make data findable and interpretable. Examples: file creation date, author name, image resolution, data format version, column headers in a CSV.

📁 File-Based & Directory-Based Structures

Files are stored in a flat structure (all in one location) or organised into directories (folders). Easy for individual file management. Suitable for small-scale systems. Limited search and query capabilities.

🏠 Hierarchy-Based Structure

Data organised in a tree structure with parent-child relationships. Each child has exactly one parent. Fast retrieval when structure is known. Used in XML, DNS, and file systems. Limitation: cannot model many-to-many relationships.

Relationship: The choice of storage structure directly impacts how data can be transformed and analysed. Relational structures support complex queries; hierarchical structures support fast path-based lookups.

Big Data & the 6 Vs (6.7.1–6.7.3)

Definition: Big Data refers to datasets so large and complex that traditional data processing tools cannot handle them efficiently. It is characterised by multiple dimensions (the 6 Vs).
DimensionDefinitionImpact on Gathering/Maintaining
📊 VolumeThe sheer quantity of data being generatedRequires scalable cloud storage; traditional databases insufficient
🌞 VarietyDifferent types (structured, unstructured) and formats (CSV, JSON, images, video)Requires flexible storage solutions (data lakes) and varied processing tools
🌕 VariabilityData flow and meaning change over time; inconsistency in dataRequires robust data quality checking and schema flexibility
⚡ VelocityThe speed at which data is generated and needs to be processedRequires real-time streaming systems (e.g. Apache Kafka)
✅ VeracityThe accuracy, reliability and trustworthiness of dataRequires validation and quality assurance processes
💰 ValueThe business value or insight that can be extracted from dataRequires analytics tools; not all Big Data is equally valuable

Factors Affecting Data Maintenance (6.7.5)

Time — ongoing effort required to keep data current Skills — specialist expertise needed for complex datasets Cost — storage, tools, and staff time are all expenses

Data Wrangling (6.8.1–6.8.2)

Definition: Data wrangling (also called data munging) is the process of transforming and mapping raw data into a more useful format for analysis.
  1. Structure — organise data into a consistent schema (e.g. define columns and data types)
  2. Clean — remove errors, duplicates, null values, and outliers
  3. Validate — apply rules to confirm data meets quality standards
  4. Enrich — augment data with additional context (e.g. join with another dataset)
  5. Output — export data in the required format for analysis or storage

Data Entry Errors & Prevention (6.8.4–6.8.5)

Types of Error

  • Transcription errors — mistakes when copying data (wrong character entered, e.g. typing 5 instead of S)
  • Transposition errors — characters entered in wrong order (e.g. 15 instead of 51; form instead of from)

Prevention Methods (6.8.5)

  • Validation of user input (type, range, format checks)
  • Verification by double entry (same data entered twice and compared)
  • Drop-down menus (restricts choices, eliminates free-text errors)
  • Pre-filled data entry boxes (reduces keystrokes needed)

Data Models (6.10)

🏠 Hierarchical Model

Tree structure with parent-child relationships. Each record has one parent. Fast for known path queries. Limitation: cannot represent many-to-many relationships. Represented with blocks, arrows and labels.

🕷 Network Model

Records linked by pointers; supports many-to-many relationships. More flexible than hierarchical. More complex to implement and navigate. Represented with blocks, arrows and labels.

📄 Relational Model

Data stored in tables (relations) with rows and columns. Relationships via primary and foreign keys. Most widely used model. Supports SQL queries. Represented with tables, rows, columns and labels.

Model Selection Factors (6.10.2)

FactorBest Model
Efficiency of accessing individual recordsHierarchical (direct path) or Relational (indexed)
Efficiency of storageRelational (normalisation eliminates redundancy)
Level of complexity in implementationHierarchical (simple) < Relational < Network (complex)
Many-to-many relationships neededNetwork or Relational (junction table)

  • Privileges — specific rights (e.g. read, write, execute, delete)

Data Analysis Tools (6.12)

Storing Big Data for Analysis

🏢 Data Warehouse

Structured repository for large volumes of processed, structured data from multiple sources. Optimised for analytical queries (OLAP). Examples: Amazon Redshift, Snowflake, Google BigQuery.

🌊 Data Lake

Stores raw data in any format (structured, unstructured, semi-structured) at massive scale. More flexible than a warehouse. Risk of becoming a "data swamp" without governance. Examples: AWS S3, Azure Data Lake.

🏖 Data Mart

A subset of a data warehouse focused on a specific business area or department (e.g. sales data mart, HR data mart). Smaller, faster to query.

Network Topologies (7.3.4)

⭐ Star

All devices connect to a central switch. Pro: one device failure doesn't affect others; easy to add/remove devices. Con: central switch failure kills the whole network; more cabling required.

🕸️ Mesh

Every device connects to every other. Pro: extremely resilient; multiple redundant paths. Con: very expensive to install and maintain; complex configuration.

🏔 Tree (Hierarchical)

Hierarchical arrangement of star networks connected via a backbone. Pro: scalable; easy to expand. Con: backbone failure affects whole branch; complex.

Logical vs Physical

Physical topology = actual cable layout. Logical topology = how data flows. A physically star network can operate logically as a bus (e.g. Ethernet over star wiring).

ENVIRONMENTS · 7.3.7–7.3.9

OSI Model & Data Packets

The 7-Layer OSI Model (7.3.7)

7 — APPLICATION LAYER

User-facing services. Protocols: HTTP, HTTPS, FTP, SFTP, SMTP, DNS, DHCP

6 — PRESENTATION LAYER

Data translation, encryption/decryption, compression. Ensures data is in usable format (e.g. SSL/TLS, JPEG, ASCII, UTF-8)

5 — SESSION LAYER

Manages sessions (opening, maintaining, closing connections between devices). Protocols: NetBIOS, RPC

4 — TRANSPORT LAYER

End-to-end data transfer; flow control; error correction. TCP (reliable, ordered) · UDP (fast, no guarantee)

3 — NETWORK LAYER

Logical addressing and packet routing between networks. Protocols: IP, ICMP, RIP, OSPF

2 — DATA LINK LAYER

Node-to-node data transfer; error detection (CRC); MAC addressing. Protocols: Ethernet, Wi-Fi (802.11), PPP

1 — PHYSICAL LAYER

Actual physical transmission of bits via cables, fibre, radio waves. Defines voltages, timing, and connector types

Data Packets (7.3.9)

Packet Structure

ComponentContents
HeaderSource IP, destination IP, packet number, protocol, TTL (time to live)
PayloadThe actual data being transmitted (a fragment of the original message)
Trailer / FooterError checking data (CRC — Cyclic Redundancy Check)

Packet Switching

Data is broken into packets that travel independently across the network, potentially via different routes. At the destination, packets are reassembled in the correct order.

Causes of packet loss: network congestion, hardware failure, signal interference, buffer overflow at routers.

Error Handling — CRC

Cyclic Redundancy Check: A value calculated from the packet data by the sender and included in the trailer. The receiver recalculates the CRC — if values differ, the packet is corrupted and must be retransmitted.

Bandwidth & Latency (7.3.11)

Bandwidth

Maximum capacity of a network connection — how much data can be transmitted per second (Mbps / Gbps). More bandwidth = more data simultaneously.

Latency

The delay between sending and receiving data (measured in milliseconds). High latency causes lag. Affected by distance, hops, congestion.

SECURITY · 8.2.3 & 8.2.4

Social Engineering & Physical Threats

Social Engineering: Manipulating people into revealing confidential information or performing actions that compromise security — exploits human psychology rather than technical vulnerabilities. Often the easiest attack vector.

🎣 Phishing

Mass-targeted fraudulent emails or fake websites impersonating trusted organisations (banks, HMRC) to steal credentials or install malware. Uses urgency and fear to prompt action.

🎯 Spear Phishing

Highly targeted phishing aimed at a specific individual, using personal details (name, role, colleague names) to appear convincingly legitimate. Much higher success rate than generic phishing.

📱 Smishing

Phishing conducted via SMS text message. Fake parcel delivery notifications, bank alerts or prize messages containing malicious links or phone numbers.

📞 Vishing

Voice phishing — fraudulent phone calls posing as IT support, banks or government agencies to extract sensitive information or direct the victim to install malware.

🌐 Pharming

Redirects users from legitimate websites to fraudulent ones by corrupting DNS entries or modifying the hosts file. Victim types correct URL but lands on attacker-controlled site.

🗿 Watering Hole Attacks

Attacker compromises a website frequently visited by the target group (e.g. industry forums), infecting all visitors. Targets specific communities or organisations indirectly.

More Social Engineering & Human Threats

🔌 USB Baiting

USB drives loaded with malware left in car parks or common areas. Victim plugs it in out of curiosity, executing malicious code automatically.

🚪 Tailgating / Piggybacking

Physically following an authorised person through a secure door without individual authentication. Prevention: enforce one-person-one-badge; challenge unknown persons.

Technical Countermeasures (8.3.1)

🔥 Firewalls (8.3.2)

Filter network traffic using configurable rules. Firewall configuration rules: inbound/outbound traffic rules, traffic type rules (TCP/UDP), application rules, IP address rules. Types: packet filtering (basic); stateful inspection; application-layer (deep packet inspection).

🚨 Intrusion Detection & Prevention

Authorised simulated cyberattacks to find vulnerabilities before malicious actors do. Results in a remediation report. Ethical hacking (authorised) vs unethical hacking (unauthorised). Types: black-box, white-box, grey-box.

Dividing a network into segments to contain breaches. Virtual (VLANs), physical (separate hardware), and offline networks (air gaps — completely isolated from internet).

SECURITY · 8.4

CIA Triad, IAAA Model & Encryption

The CIA Triad (8.4.1)

🔒 Confidentiality

Ensuring that data is kept private by controlling who has access. Achieved through encryption, access controls, authentication and need-to-know principles. Breach example: data leak or unauthorised access.

✅ Integrity

Ensuring that data has not been tampered with and remains accurate. Maintaining confidentiality helps protect integrity. Breach example: man-in-the-middle modifying data in transit. Methods: hashing, digital signatures, CRC.

⏱ Availability

Ensuring data and systems are accessible and useful when needed. Maintaining integrity helps ensure availability. Breach example: DDoS, ransomware. Methods: redundancy, backups, failover systems, resilient environments.

CIA Interrelationship: Confidentiality → supports Integrity (controlling access prevents tampering) → supports Availability (uncorrupted data can be accessed reliably). All three must be maintained simultaneously.

Encryption

🔑 Symmetric Encryption

Same key used to encrypt and decrypt. Fast and efficient for large data. Problem: how to securely share the key. Examples: AES (128/256-bit), DES (legacy, now weak).

🔐 Asymmetric Encryption

Uses a public key (shared openly) to encrypt and a private key (kept secret) to decrypt. Solves the key exchange problem. Slower than symmetric. Examples: RSA, ECC. Used in HTTPS handshake.

🔒 TLS / HTTPS

Transport Layer Security encrypts data in transit between client and server using asymmetric encryption to establish session, then symmetric for data transfer. HTTPS = HTTP + TLS. Prevents eavesdropping and MitM.

Hashing: One-way mathematical function producing a fixed-length digest. Cannot be reversed. Used for storing passwords safely (never store plaintext). If hashes match, passwords match. Examples: SHA-256, bcrypt. Not encryption — hashing is irreversible.

The IAAA Model (8.4.2)

1️⃣ Identification

Recognising who the user claims to be within a digital system. Methods:

  • Knowledge-based: username, email address
  • Possession-based: smart card, token, key fob
  • Biometric-based: fingerprint scan, facial recognition

2️⃣ Authentication

Verifying the identity claimed during identification. Methods:

  • Multi-factor authentication (MFA) — combines 2+ factors for stronger verification
  • Passwords and passphrases — knowledge factor
  • Biometric authentication — fingerprint, face, iris scan

3️⃣ Authorisation

Ensuring authenticated users can only access resources they are permitted to. Implements least privilege. Methods:

  • Role-based authorisation (RBAC) — permissions tied to job role
  • Access Control Lists (ACLs) — specific permissions per user/resource

4️⃣ Accountability

Ensuring all actions within a system can be traced back to the responsible user. Non-repudiation — users cannot deny their actions. Methods:

  • Audit logs — timestamped records of all system activity
  • User activity monitoring — login times, resources accessed, changes made

Authentication Factors Summary

FactorTypeExample
KnowledgeSomething you knowPassword, PIN, security question
PossessionSomething you haveAuthenticator app, hardware token, SMS OTP, smart card
InherenceSomething you areFingerprint, face ID, iris scan, voice recognition
