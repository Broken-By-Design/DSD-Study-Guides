BUSINESS · 5.1

Business Environment

🏢 Types of Organisation

  • Private sector — owned by individuals. Includes SMEs (<250 staff, ≤£44m turnover), large enterprises, NGOs (e.g. police, HMRC)
  • Public sector — government-owned, funded through taxes (NHS, state schools, councils)
  • Third sector — Charities, Community groups, Social enterprises (community goal; all profits reinvested)

🛒 Business Models

  • B2C — sell directly to private individuals via shops/websites. E.g. Sainsbury's, Spotify
  • B2B — sell only to businesses (bulk/complex products). E.g. Intel, Caterpillar, Deloitte
  • B2M — sell to both businesses and individuals. E.g. Virgin broadband

👥 Stakeholders (5.1.3)

  • Internal: Owners, Directors, Managers, Employees
  • External: Customers, Suppliers, Shareholders, Government, Investors, Outsourced services
BUSINESS · 5.2

Digital Value to Organisations

📢 Sales & Marketing

  • Online selling, loyalty schemes, social media & SEO analytics
  • Personalised offers from customer data (loyalty cards, purchase history)
  • Better customer retention via CRM and digital feedback tools

👔 HR & Operations

  • Digital recruitment platforms, HR software (payroll, performance)
  • E-learning platforms, automated workflows
  • ERP systems for real-time operational data

📊 Finance, Logistics & Management

  • Cloud accounting (Sage, Xero), real-time financial reporting
  • GPS tracking, route optimisation, Warehouse Management Systems
  • MIS with live KPI dashboards

🔬 R&D & User Needs (5.2.2)

  • AI brainstorming, 3D printing, CAD software for rapid prototyping
  • Functionality, Accessibility (WCAG), Availability, Compatibility with legacy systems
  • End-user support: digital help, documentation, training systems
BUSINESS · 5.3

Risk to Organisations

🔒 Security & Financial Risks

  • Fraud, cybercrime costs, system downtime losses, ransomware payments
  • GDPR fines up to 4% global turnover or £17.5m
  • Legal costs, compensation claims from breaches

📦 Reputational & Operational

  • Loss of customer trust, negative media coverage, share price falls
  • Over-reliance on systems creates single points of failure
  • Legacy system compatibility blocking upgrades

👤 Audience Exclusion

  • Rural communities without broadband; elderly/disabled users excluded
  • AI bias — hiring algorithms, facial recognition errors, credit-scoring bias
  • Poor UX for low-literacy users and non-native English speakers

🏛️ Rival Technologies

  • Competitors offering mobile apps, cloud solutions or digital downloads
  • Platform obsolescence (e.g. CDs replaced by streaming)
  • Failure to adopt cloud/AI gives competitors efficiency advantage

Potential Impacts

Legal actionICO / regulatory finesReputational damageLoss of customersFinancial lossWithdrawal of licence

BUSINESS · 5.4.3-5

Change Process & Feasibility

📄 Responses to Change (5.4.3)

  • New/amended policies — sustainability, GDPR, safeguarding, hybrid working
  • New/amended processes — staffing (recruit or downsize), delivery schedules
  • Introducing new hardware/software or cloud systems
  • Staff retraining and upskilling programmes

🔄 Change Management (5.4.4)

  • Identify need → plan → communicate → implement → review
  • Change Advisory Board (CAB) prioritises and approves changes
  • Benefits: reduced resistance, lower failure risk, better resource allocation
  • Drawbacks: time-consuming, costly, may face staff resistance

🔍 Feasibility Factors (5.4.5)

  • Technical — does the technology exist?
  • Economic — cost–benefit analysis, ROI, budget
  • Legal — data protection, licences, accessibility
  • Operational — impact on workflows and staff
  • Schedule — deliverable within the required time?
💡Exam tip: For feasibility questions, use all five factors (TELSO) and relate each to the specific scenario given — don't just list definitions.

DATA · 6.1

Data, Information & Knowledge

💾 Data

Raw facts and figures with no context. E.g. HN39XKC — without context it has no meaning.

📋 Information

Data contextualised to add meaning. E.g. HN39XKC is the number plate of a car seen outside a bank during a robbery.

🧠 Knowledge

The ability to understand and act from information. E.g. “The driver may be involved in bank robberies.” You cannot have knowledge without data/information.

🔢 Quantitative (Numerical)

  • Can be counted or measured numerically
  • Discrete — countable separate values (shoe sizes, dice rolls)
  • Continuous — infinite range including decimals (height, temperature)
  • Categorical — values assigned to categories (1–5 satisfaction rating)
  • Usually structured — stored in spreadsheets or databases

💬 Qualitative (Descriptive)

  • Cannot be expressed numerically — “wordy”
  • Provides detailed insight (opinions, feelings, explanations)
  • Collected via open questions, interviews, focus groups
  • Usually unstructured — harder to sort or analyse automatically
Ethical data practices (6.1.3): Collect only what is needed. Store securely. Be transparent. Respect privacy. Comply with UK GDPR. Avoid bias in collection methods.

📋 Structured Data

Predefined format in a data structure (spreadsheet, database). Can be searched, sorted and organised easily. Quantitative data is usually structured.

💬 Unstructured Data

No predefined format — harder to analyse automatically. E.g. emails, social media posts, images, videos. Requires NLP / ML tools to extract meaning.

DATA · 6.2 & 6.5

Formats & Transformation

📄 Text (.txt)

Plain unformatted text. Human-readable, no structure. Used for simple logs and notes.

📊 CSV

Each row = one record; commas separate fields. Simple, portable, opens in Excel. No formatting support.

🌐 XML

Tags define document structure. Good schema support. Used by legacy systems. More verbose than JSON.

{ JSON

Easy to read/write. Used by modern web APIs. Converts to objects in most programming languages.

🗃 Database

Best for large structured data with complex queries and relationships. Multi-user access. Strong data integrity.

🔁 ASCII vs UTF-8

ASCII: 1 byte/char, English only. UTF-8: multilingual, supports all Unicode. Use UTF-8 for modern apps.

Methods of Transforming Data (6.2.1)

DATA · 6.6 & 6.7

Storage & Big Data

🔍 Metadata

Data about data — file size, creation date, author, GPS coordinates. Helps find and manage files without opening them.

📁 File / Directory Storage

Files stored in a hierarchy (root → subfolders → files). Simple but no relationships between data; hard to search large volumes.

💾 Storage Hardware

  • HDD — magnetic spinning platters; cheap per GB, slower, less durable
  • SSD — flash memory; faster, more durable, more expensive per GB
  • Cloud — remote servers; accessible anywhere, pay-as-you-go

DATA · 6.7 & 6.8

Data Quality & Systems

✅ Accuracy

Data reflects reality — no errors or incorrect values. Inaccurate data leads to wrong decisions.

📋 Completeness

No missing required values. Gaps cause analysis to fail or produce partial, unreliable results.

🔄 Consistency

Same format and value across all systems. E.g. dates stored as DD/MM/YYYY everywhere.

⏰ Timeliness

Data is up to date when needed. Stale data leads to outdated and incorrect decisions.

🔢 Validity

Data conforms to defined rules — e.g. age must be a positive integer; email must contain @.

🚫 Data Entry Errors

  • Transcription — wrong character typed
  • Transposition — digits swapped (13 entered as 31)
  • Omission — field left blank
  • Duplication — same record entered twice

🛠️ Validation & Verification

  • Input masks, drop-down lists, range/type/presence/format checks
  • Verification: double-entry, visual check against source
  • Data wrangling — cleaning and transforming raw data into a usable format

Data Models & Analysis Tools (6.10 & 6.12)

🗃 Flat File

Single table (like a spreadsheet). Simple but causes data duplication — no relationships between records.

🔗 Relational Model

Multiple linked tables using primary keys and foreign keys. Reduces duplication. Supports SQL. E.g. MySQL, PostgreSQL.

📄 NoSQL / Document

Flexible schema; stores JSON-like documents. Good for unstructured or rapidly changing data. E.g. MongoDB.

📊 Analysis Tools (6.12)

  • Spreadsheets: Excel / Google Sheets
  • Python / R: large-scale statistical analysis
  • BI Tools: Power BI, Tableau (dashboards)
  • SQL: relational database querying
Data access (6.11): APIs allow data exchange between platforms. REST APIs use HTTP, authenticated with API keys or OAuth tokens.

ENVIRONMENTS · 7.1 & 7.2

Hardware & Software

💻 Desktop

Separate monitor, keyboard, mouse. More power, expandable. Fixed location.

💻 Laptop

Portable. Built-in trackpad & keyboard. Lower-power mobile CPU. Battery powered.

📱 Tablet / Smartphone

Touchscreen interface. Very portable. Smartphone has GPS, camera, sensors, always connected.

🖥 Server

High-spec, runs 24/7. Hosts websites, files, databases. Often virtualised. Rack-mounted in data centres.

🔌 Embedded / IoT

Single-purpose computers inside products (cars, smart meters). Low power, dedicated function.

⚙️ Internal Components

  • CPU — processes instructions (“the brain”)
  • RAM — temporary working memory; lost on shutdown
  • GPU — processes graphics and parallel computations
  • Motherboard — connects all components
  • PSU — power supply unit
  • NIC — network interface card

🛠️ Software Categories (7.2)

  • OS — Windows, macOS, Linux, Android, iOS; RTOS for real-time critical systems
  • Utilities — File managers, defragmenters, compression (7-Zip), antivirus, backup software
  • Dev tools — IDEs (VS Code), compilers, debuggers, Git version control
  • Applications — Word, Excel, Outlook, Chrome, Teams, Zoom
ENVIRONMENTS · 7.3

Networks

🟢 Benefits

  • Share files, printers and resources centrally
  • Centralised backups — reduces risk of data loss
  • Share software licences — reduces cost
  • Easier central management and updates

🔴 Drawbacks

  • Expensive to set up (servers, cabling, routers)
  • Malware can spread quickly across the whole network
  • Server failure affects all connected users simultaneously

Types of Networks (7.3.2)

Network Models & Topologies

⭐ Star

All devices connect to a central switch/hub. Most common. Easy to add devices. Switch failure = all down.

🚀 Bus

All devices on one cable. Simple and cheap. If cable fails, whole network fails. Not scalable.

🔗 Mesh

Every device connected to every other. Very resilient — multiple paths. Expensive and complex.

🖥 Client–Server

Dedicated server provides services to clients. Centralised control and security. Servers are expensive.

👤 P2P

All devices equal — share resources directly. No dedicated server. Cheap but hard to manage.

ENVIRONMENTS · 7.3.7-11

OSI Model & Protocols

The 7-Layer OSI Model (7.3.7)

#LayerFunction & Protocols
7ApplicationHTTP/S, FTP, SMTP, DNS — user-facing protocols
6PresentationEncryption, compression, format conversion (SSL/TLS)
5SessionManages sessions/connections between applications
4TransportTCP (reliable) or UDP (fast). Port numbers for services.
3NetworkIP addressing and routing between networks
2Data LinkMAC addresses; error detection on a single link
1PhysicalBits over cables, Wi-Fi signals, fibre optic light

Key Protocols & Concepts

🌐 Protocols

  • HTTP/HTTPS — web pages; HTTPS encrypted with TLS/SSL
  • FTP — file transfer between computers
  • DNS — translates domain names to IP addresses
  • DHCP — automatically assigns IP addresses to devices
  • SMTP / IMAP / POP3 — sending and receiving email
  • TCP — reliable ordered delivery; UDP — faster, no guarantee

🔌 Packets, Bandwidth & Latency

  • Data broken into packets: header (IP addresses, packet number) + payload + trailer (error check)
  • Packets may take different routes and are reassembled at destination
  • Bandwidth — max data per second (Mbps/Gbps)
  • Latency — delay between send and receive (ms); high latency bad for gaming/video calls
ENVIRONMENTS · 7.4 & 7.5

Cloud & Virtualisation

💻 Virtual Machines (7.4.1)

  • Software-based emulation of a physical computer; runs its own OS
  • Managed by a Hypervisor which allocates CPU, RAM, storage, networking
  • Type 1 (bare metal) — runs directly on hardware; fastest (e.g. VMware ESXi)
  • Type 2 (hosted) — runs on top of an OS (e.g. VirtualBox)

⚖️ VM Benefits & Drawbacks

  • ✓ Multiple VMs on one server — saves cost
  • ✓ Multiple OS on one machine for testing
  • ✓ Snapshots for backup and quick recovery
  • ✗ High initial server cost
  • ✗ If hypervisor fails, all VMs go down
  • ✗ Some latency overhead vs physical hardware

Cloud Service Models (7.5)

🌐 IaaS

Infrastructure as a Service. Rent virtual hardware. Customer manages OS and apps. E.g. AWS EC2, Azure VMs.

💻 PaaS

Platform as a Service. Development environment provided. Customer manages apps only. E.g. Google App Engine, Heroku.

📱 SaaS

Software as a Service. Software via browser; provider manages everything. E.g. Microsoft 365, Salesforce, Zoom.

🏠 Private Cloud

Single organisation; more control and security; more expensive. E.g. own on-premises data centre.

🌐 Public Cloud

Shared infrastructure; pay-as-you-go; highly scalable. E.g. AWS, Azure, Google Cloud.

📉 Cloud Benefits

  • Scalability & flexibility
  • Reduced upfront hardware costs
  • Remote access from anywhere
  • Automatic updates & built-in disaster recovery
ENVIRONMENTS · 7.6

Resilient Environments

✅ Why Resilience Matters (7.6.1)

  • Minimises downtime — services stay available to customers
  • Protects against data loss from hardware failure or attack
  • Enables disaster recovery and business continuity
  • Maintains customer trust and regulatory compliance
  • Reduces financial losses from unplanned outages

🛠️ Improving Resilience (7.6.2)

  • Redundant hardware — backup servers, RAID storage arrays
  • Failover systems — automatic switch to backup if primary fails
  • Load balancing — spread traffic across multiple servers
  • Regular backups — on-site and off-site/cloud (3-2-1 rule)
  • UPS (Uninterruptible Power Supply) — power cut protection
  • Disaster Recovery Plan (DRP) — documented restoration steps
  • Geographic redundancy — data centres in multiple locations
3-2-1 Backup Rule: Keep 3 copies of data, on 2 different media types, with 1 stored off-site or in the cloud. Test restores regularly — untested backups are not reliable backups.

SECURITY · 8.1

Security Risks

🔒 Confidential Data (8.1.1–8.1.2)

  • HR data: salaries, bonuses, personal details, legal disputes
  • Commercial: client details, contracts, pricing, profit margins
  • IP / Trade secrets: product designs, secret recipes (KFC, Coca-Cola), algorithms (Google Search)
  • Strategic plans: upcoming restructuring, new products, partnerships

📈 Impact of Security Failures (8.1.3)

  • Financial: fraud, ransomware payments, GDPR fines (£17.5m or 4% global turnover)
  • Reputational: loss of trust, negative media, share price falls
  • Operational: system downtime, data corruption, inability to serve customers
  • Legal: prosecution under Computer Misuse Act 1990, UK GDPR, DPA 2018

Vulnerabilities

🔐 Technical

  • Unpatched software; outdated OS
  • Weak/default passwords; misconfigured firewalls
  • Insecure code (SQL injection, XSS)

👤 Human

  • Phishing clicks, credential sharing
  • Tailgating through secure doors
  • Shoulder surfing, poor password hygiene

🏠 Physical

  • Device theft (laptops, USB drives)
  • Dumpster diving for sensitive documents
  • Leaving machines unlocked and unattended

SECURITY · 8.3

Threat Mitigation

🔑 Access Control & Auth

  • Passwords with complexity requirements
  • MFA — something you know + have/are
  • Biometrics, smart cards, TOTP apps
  • Role-Based Access Control (RBAC)
  • Principle of least privilege

🔒 Encryption

  • Symmetric — same key to encrypt/decrypt; faster
  • Asymmetric — public/private key pair; more secure
  • Used for data in transit (HTTPS/TLS) and at rest
  • Hash functions (SHA-256) — irreversible fingerprint for integrity checking

🖥 Firewalls & Antivirus

  • Hardware or software filtering network traffic
  • Rules determine allowed inbound/outbound traffic
  • Antivirus scans for known malware signatures in real time
  • Patch management — apply software updates regularly

🔏 Network Security

  • Network segregation — VLANs limit spread of attacks
  • Air gap — completely offline network; very secure
  • Network monitoring — alert on suspicious traffic; audit logs
  • Port scanning — identify open ports (offensive and defensive)

💾 Backups & Policies

  • 3-2-1 rule: 3 copies, 2 media types, 1 off-site; test restores
  • Clear desk policy, screen lock, device encryption
  • Staff phishing awareness training and simulated tests
  • Penetration testing — ethical hackers simulate attacks to find weaknesses
NCSC Password Advice: Use three random words as a passphrase (e.g. coffee-staple-dragon). Store all passwords in a password manager. Never reuse passwords across accounts.
SECURITY · 8.4

CIA Triad & IAAA Model

🔒 Confidentiality

Information only accessible by those authorised. Protected by: encryption, access controls, need-to-know policies. Closely linked to UK GDPR.

✅ Integrity

Data must be accurate and not tampered with. Protected by: hashing, digital signatures, access controls, audit logs. Wrong data leads to wrong decisions.

📈 Availability

Systems and data accessible to authorised users when needed. Protected by: redundancy, failover, backups, DDoS mitigation, UPS. A DDoS attack directly targets Availability.

CIA & GDPR: The CIA triad aligns closely with UK GDPR and DPA 2018. Organisations have a legal duty to protect personal data under all three principles. (Also called the AIC triad to avoid confusion with Central Intelligence Agency.)

The IAAA Model (8.4.2)

StepWhat It MeansExample
IdentificationClaiming an identity — not yet verifiedEntering a username or employee ID
AuthenticationProving you are who you claim to bePassword, biometric scan, TOTP code, smart card
AuthorisationDetermining what the authenticated user is permitted to do (RBAC)Admin can delete; standard user can only view
Accounting (Audit)Logging and monitoring user actions — creates an audit trailWho accessed which file, when and from where
💡Exam tip: Know all four IAAA stages in order and give specific techniques for each. Know how CIA and IAAA interrelate — Authentication supports Confidentiality; Audit logs support Integrity; MFA supports all three pillars of CIA.
